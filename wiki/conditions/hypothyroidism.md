# Hypothyroidism (Primary)

## Summary

A common endocrine disorder in which the thyroid gland produces insufficient
thyroid hormone, raising TSH. In India the leading cause is autoimmune
(Hashimoto) thyroiditis; iodine status also plays a role. Often presents
insidiously with non-specific symptoms.

## Current Guideline

- Confirm with TSH; add free T4 when TSH is abnormal.
- Treat overt hypothyroidism (high TSH, low free T4) with levothyroxine.
- For subclinical hypothyroidism (high TSH, normal free T4), individualise:
  treat if TSH persistently > 10 mIU/L, symptomatic, positive anti-TPO, or
  planning pregnancy.
- Recheck TSH 6–8 weeks after starting or changing the dose; titrate to target.

## Diagnostic Criteria

- Elevated TSH with low free T4 = overt primary hypothyroidism.
- Elevated TSH with normal free T4 = subclinical hypothyroidism.
- Anti-TPO antibodies support autoimmune aetiology.
- Symptoms: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin,
  menorrhagia, low mood, bradycardia.

## Treatment

- **Levothyroxine** once daily on an empty stomach, 30–60 min before breakfast.
- Typical full replacement ≈ 1.6 µg/kg/day; start low (25–50 µg) in elderly or
  cardiac patients and uptitrate.
- Target TSH within the reference range (and trimester-specific targets in
  pregnancy).
- Lifelong therapy with periodic monitoring; do not stop when symptoms resolve.

## Drug Interactions

- Calcium, iron, and antacids reduce levothyroxine absorption — separate doses
  by at least 4 hours.
- Levothyroxine can increase warfarin effect and may raise insulin/oral
  hypoglycaemic requirements in patients with `[[conditions/type-2-diabetes]]`.
- Proton-pump inhibitors and high-fibre meals may reduce absorption.

## Red Flags

- Myxoedema coma: hypothermia, altered consciousness, bradycardia → emergency.
- New angina or arrhythmia after starting levothyroxine → reduce dose, review.
- Pregnancy: under-treatment risks the fetus — escalate dosing promptly and
  refer for trimester-specific targets.
