# Migraine

## Summary

A common primary headache disorder of recurrent moderate-to-severe headache,
often unilateral and pulsating, with nausea and sensitivity to light and sound.
May occur with or without aura. A leading cause of disability in young adults,
especially women.

## Current Guideline

- Diagnose clinically using ICHD-3 criteria; neuroimaging is not routine in
  typical migraine with a normal exam.
- Treat acute attacks early with simple analgesia or triptans; avoid overuse.
- Offer preventive therapy when attacks are frequent (≥ 4 headache days/month),
  disabling, or acute treatment is overused.
- Address lifestyle triggers and counsel on medication-overuse headache.

## Diagnostic Criteria

- At least 5 attacks lasting 4–72 hours with ≥ 2 of: unilateral, pulsating,
  moderate–severe intensity, aggravated by routine activity; **and** ≥ 1 of
  nausea/vomiting or photophobia + phonophobia.
- Aura (if present): reversible visual/sensory/speech symptoms developing over
  ≥ 5 min and lasting < 60 min.
- Red-flag features absent (see below).

## Treatment

- **Acute:** NSAID (e.g. naproxen/ibuprofen) or paracetamol for mild; triptans
  (e.g. sumatriptan) for moderate–severe; add an antiemetic for nausea.
- Limit acute medication to < 10–15 days/month to prevent overuse headache.
- **Preventive:** propranolol, amitriptyline, or topiramate are common
  first-line options; titrate slowly and review at 8–12 weeks.
- Non-drug: hydration, regular sleep, trigger diary, stress management.

## Drug Interactions

- Triptans + SSRIs/SNRIs: monitor for serotonin syndrome.
- Triptans are contraindicated with ergots and in uncontrolled
  `[[conditions/hypertension]]` or ischaemic heart disease.
- Propranolol can mask hypoglycaemia and worsen asthma/bradycardia.
- Topiramate reduces efficacy of combined oral contraceptives and predisposes
  to renal stones; amitriptyline adds anticholinergic load.

## Red Flags

- Sudden "thunderclap" headache → exclude subarachnoid haemorrhage, emergency.
- New headache with fever and neck stiffness → suspect meningitis.
- Focal neurological deficit, papilloedema, or progressive worsening → image and
  refer.
- New headache after age 50, or in immunocompromised/cancer patients → escalate.
