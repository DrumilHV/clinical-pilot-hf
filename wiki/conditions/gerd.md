# Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

## Summary

Troublesome symptoms or complications from reflux of stomach contents into the
oesophagus. Typical features are heartburn and acid regurgitation. Very common
in OPD; mostly managed with lifestyle measures and acid suppression, but
alarm features require endoscopy.

## Current Guideline

- Diagnose clinically in typical, uncomplicated cases and offer an empirical PPI
  trial.
- Endoscope first if alarm features are present (see Red Flags) or symptoms are
  refractory.
- Use the lowest effective PPI dose for the shortest necessary duration; step
  down after symptom control.
- Emphasise lifestyle and dietary modification as the foundation of management.

## Diagnostic Criteria

- Classic symptoms: heartburn and regurgitation, often postprandial or nocturnal.
- Response to a PPI trial supports the diagnosis.
- Upper GI endoscopy for alarm features or to assess for oesophagitis,
  stricture, or Barrett's oesophagus.
- 24-hour pH-impedance monitoring reserved for diagnostic uncertainty or
  pre-surgical evaluation.

## Treatment

- **Lifestyle:** weight loss, smaller meals, avoid late-night eating, elevate
  the head of the bed, reduce trigger foods (fatty/spicy/caffeine), stop smoking
  and limit alcohol.
- **Pharmacological:** PPI (e.g. omeprazole/pantoprazole) once daily before
  breakfast for 4–8 weeks; antacids/alginates for breakthrough symptoms.
- H2-receptor antagonists are an alternative or add-on for nocturnal symptoms.
- Refer for anti-reflux surgery in selected refractory cases.

## Drug Interactions

- Omeprazole/esomeprazole inhibit CYP2C19 and can reduce activation of
  clopidogrel — prefer pantoprazole when clopidogrel is needed.
- PPIs reduce absorption of drugs needing gastric acidity (e.g. ketoconazole,
  some iron salts) and may lower magnesium with long-term use.
- Antacids impair absorption of many drugs (levothyroxine, fluoroquinolones,
  iron) — separate dosing.

## Red Flags

- Dysphagia, odynophagia, or food sticking → urgent endoscopy.
- Unintentional weight loss, anaemia, or GI bleeding (haematemesis, melaena) →
  escalate.
- Persistent vomiting, or symptoms unresponsive to adequate PPI therapy.
- New-onset reflux symptoms after age 50 → endoscopy.
