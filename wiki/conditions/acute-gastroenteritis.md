# Acute Gastroenteritis (Adult)

## Summary

Acute inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract causing diarrhoea (≥ 3 loose
stools/day), often with vomiting, abdominal cramps, and fever. Most cases are
self-limiting and viral; the central priority is **assessment and correction of
dehydration**. Antibiotics are reserved for specific indications, in keeping
with antimicrobial stewardship.

## Current Guideline

- Classify dehydration as none/mild, some/moderate, or severe and rehydrate
  accordingly — **oral rehydration solution (ORS)** is first-line for mild–
  moderate cases.
- Do **not** give routine antibiotics for acute watery diarrhoea; most resolve
  without them.
- Continue feeding; avoid prolonged fasting.
- Reserve antimicrobials for dysentery (bloody stool), suspected cholera with
  severe dehydration, confirmed parasitic infection, or immunocompromised/septic
  patients.

## Diagnostic Criteria

- Clinical diagnosis: acute onset of ≥ 3 loose/watery stools per day,
  ± vomiting, cramps, or fever, typically < 14 days.
- Assess hydration: thirst, skin turgor, mucous membranes, pulse, blood
  pressure, urine output, and mental status.
- Stool studies (microscopy, culture) only when there is blood/mucus, severe or
  persistent illness, recent travel, an outbreak, or immunosuppression.
- Check electrolytes and renal function in moderate–severe dehydration.

## Treatment

- **Rehydration:** ORS for mild–moderate dehydration; IV isotonic fluids for
  severe dehydration, persistent vomiting, or shock.
- **Zinc** supplementation has an established role in paediatric diarrhoea; in
  adults focus on fluids and electrolytes.
- **Symptomatic:** antiemetics for significant vomiting; antimotility agents
  (loperamide) only in non-bloody, afebrile diarrhoea and avoided in suspected
  invasive infection.
- **Antibiotics (only when indicated):** a fluoroquinolone or azithromycin for
  dysentery/invasive disease per local sensitivity; metronidazole for confirmed
  amoebiasis/giardiasis.
- Reinforce hand hygiene, safe water, and food safety to prevent spread.

## Drug Interactions

- Loperamide + other CNS depressants → additive sedation; avoid high doses
  (cardiac risk).
- Fluoroquinolones bind divalent cations in antacids, iron, and ORS-unrelated
  supplements — separate dosing.
- Metronidazole + alcohol → disulfiram-like reaction; counsel abstinence during
  and shortly after the course.
- Ondansetron + other QT-prolonging agents → monitor in at-risk patients.

## Red Flags

- Signs of severe dehydration or hypovolaemic shock.
- Bloody diarrhoea, high fever, or severe abdominal pain (consider invasive or
  surgical cause).
- Persistent vomiting preventing oral rehydration.
- Diarrhoea > 14 days, or illness in the elderly, pregnant, or immunocompromised
  — lower threshold for investigation and referral.
