# Community-Acquired Pneumonia (Adult)

## Summary

Acute infection of the lung parenchyma acquired outside a healthcare setting,
presenting with fever, cough, sputum, breathlessness, and pleuritic chest pain.
Severity ranges from outpatient-manageable to life-threatening. Empirical
antibiotic choice is guided by severity, comorbidity, and local resistance
patterns; rational antibiotic use is essential to limit antimicrobial
resistance.

## Current Guideline

- Assess severity at presentation using a structured score (e.g., **CURB-65**)
  together with clinical judgement to decide outpatient vs inpatient vs ICU
  care.
- Start empirical antibiotics promptly after diagnosis; do not delay for
  investigations in moderate–severe disease.
- De-escalate and narrow therapy once culture/sensitivity and clinical response
  are known.
- Typical total duration is **5 days** for uncomplicated CAP that responds, and
  the patient should be afebrile and clinically stable for 48–72 hours before
  stopping.

## Diagnostic Criteria

- Clinical: new/worsening cough with at least one of fever, purulent sputum,
  pleuritic pain, or dyspnoea, plus focal chest signs.
- Radiology: new infiltrate/consolidation on chest X-ray supports the diagnosis.
- Investigations by severity: pulse oximetry for all; CBC, urea/electrolytes,
  CRP, blood and sputum cultures for those admitted.
- **CURB-65** (1 point each): Confusion, Urea > 7 mmol/L, Respiratory rate ≥ 30,
  BP < 90 systolic or ≤ 60 diastolic, Age ≥ 65. Score 0–1 generally outpatient,
  2 short inpatient, ≥ 3 assess for ICU.

## Treatment

- **Outpatient, no comorbidity:** oral amoxicillin; a macrolide (azithromycin)
  where atypical cover or penicillin allergy applies.
- **Outpatient with comorbidity:** amoxicillin-clavulanate ± a macrolide.
- **Inpatient (non-ICU):** IV beta-lactam (amoxicillin-clavulanate or a
  third-generation cephalosporin) + a macrolide.
- **Severe/ICU:** IV beta-lactam + macrolide (or respiratory fluoroquinolone),
  with cover broadened only if risk factors for resistant organisms exist.
- Supportive care: oxygen to keep SpO2 ≥ 92% (88–92% if chronic CO2 retention),
  fluids, antipyretics, and early mobilisation.

## Drug Interactions

- Macrolides (azithromycin, clarithromycin) prolong the QT interval — caution
  with other QT-prolonging drugs and in electrolyte disturbance.
- Clarithromycin is a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor (raises levels of statins,
  some calcium-channel blockers, warfarin).
- Fluoroquinolones reduce the seizure threshold, interact with divalent cations
  (antacids, iron, calcium) reducing absorption, and may potentiate warfarin.
- Beta-lactam allergy: verify the nature of any penicillin allergy before
  prescribing cephalosporins.

## Red Flags

- SpO2 < 92% on room air, respiratory rate ≥ 30, or rising oxygen requirement.
- Haemodynamic instability / sepsis (hypotension, lactate rise, altered
  sensorium).
- CURB-65 ≥ 3, or clinical deterioration despite 48–72 hours of therapy.
- Suspected complications: empyema, lung abscess, or multilobar consolidation.
