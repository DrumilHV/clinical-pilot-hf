# Urinary Tract Infection (Uncomplicated)

## Summary

Infection of the lower urinary tract (cystitis), most commonly caused by
*Escherichia coli*. Predominantly affects women. "Uncomplicated" means a
non-pregnant adult with a structurally and functionally normal urinary tract.
Recurrent or complicated infection warrants further evaluation.

## Current Guideline

- Treat uncomplicated cystitis empirically based on local resistance patterns;
  do not wait for culture in a typical presentation.
- Reserve fluoroquinolones for pyelonephritis or when first-line agents are
  unsuitable — antimicrobial stewardship is a priority given high resistance in
  India.
- Send a urine culture before antibiotics in pregnancy, recurrent UTI,
  treatment failure, or suspected pyelonephritis.
- Encourage adequate hydration; routine urine alkalinisers are not essential.

## Diagnostic Criteria

- Dysuria, urinary frequency, urgency, and suprapubic discomfort without vaginal
  discharge strongly suggests cystitis.
- Urine dipstick positive for nitrites and/or leukocyte esterase supports the
  diagnosis.
- Midstream urine microscopy: pyuria (≥ 10 WBC/µL); culture ≥ 10⁵ CFU/mL
  confirms (lower thresholds count with typical symptoms).
- Flank pain, fever, rigors, or vomiting point to upper-tract involvement
  (pyelonephritis).

## Treatment

- **First-line (uncomplicated cystitis):** nitrofurantoin 100 mg BD for 5 days,
  or fosfomycin 3 g single dose, where locally available and susceptible.
- **Alternatives:** co-trimoxazole only if local resistance is low.
- **Pyelonephritis:** culture-guided therapy; consider admission if vomiting,
  sepsis, or pregnancy.
- Symptomatic relief: analgesia and hydration.
- Patients with diabetes (`[[conditions/type-2-diabetes]]`) need closer
  follow-up as infections can be more severe.

## Drug Interactions

- Nitrofurantoin: avoid in significant renal impairment (eGFR < 45) and at term
  in pregnancy (risk of neonatal haemolysis).
- Co-trimoxazole potentiates warfarin and sulfonylureas, and raises potassium
  with ACE inhibitors/ARBs.
- Fluoroquinolones reduce absorption when taken with calcium/iron/antacids and
  increase tendon-rupture risk with corticosteroids.

## Red Flags

- Fever, flank pain, or rigors → suspected pyelonephritis, escalate.
- Pregnancy with any UTI (including asymptomatic bacteriuria) → treat and
  culture.
- Haematuria persisting after treatment, or recurrent infections → investigate
  for stones, obstruction, or malignancy.
- Signs of sepsis (hypotension, confusion, tachycardia) → urgent referral.
