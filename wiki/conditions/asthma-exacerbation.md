# Asthma Exacerbation (Acute, Adult)

## Summary

An acute or subacute worsening of asthma symptoms — breathlessness, wheeze,
cough, chest tightness — with a measurable fall in airflow. Exacerbations are
medical emergencies on a spectrum; rapid assessment of severity guides
treatment. The cornerstones are repeated inhaled bronchodilators, early systemic
corticosteroids, and controlled oxygen.

## Current Guideline

- Assess severity immediately using speech, work of breathing, respiratory rate,
  heart rate, oxygen saturation, and PEF where feasible.
- Give **inhaled short-acting beta-agonist (SABA)** early and repeatedly,
  preferably via a spacer in mild–moderate cases or nebuliser in severe cases.
- Start **systemic corticosteroids** within the first hour for all but the
  mildest exacerbations.
- Titrate **controlled oxygen** to a target SpO2 of **93–95%** — avoid routine
  high-flow oxygen.
- Review and step up maintenance inhaled-corticosteroid therapy before
  discharge; do not discharge on SABA alone.

## Diagnostic Criteria

- Known or newly suspected asthma with acute worsening of symptoms and reduced
  airflow.
- **Severity markers:**
  - *Mild–moderate:* talks in phrases, not agitated, RR raised but < 30,
    SpO2 90–95%, PEF > 50% predicted/best.
  - *Severe:* talks in words, agitated, RR ≥ 30, HR > 120, SpO2 < 90%,
    PEF ≤ 50%.
  - *Life-threatening:* drowsiness, confusion, silent chest, poor respiratory
    effort, cyanosis, bradycardia, or exhaustion.
- A "normal" or rising PaCO2 in a tiring patient is an ominous sign.

## Treatment

- **Bronchodilator:** SABA (salbutamol) repeated every 20 min in the first hour;
  add ipratropium bromide in severe exacerbations.
- **Corticosteroid:** oral prednisolone (or IV hydrocortisone if oral not
  tolerated), typically a short course of about 5 days.
- **Oxygen:** controlled, titrated to SpO2 93–95%.
- **Severe/life-threatening:** consider IV magnesium sulfate; arrange critical
  care review; prepare for possible ventilatory support.
- **Before discharge:** confirm improving PEF, ensure inhaler technique, start or
  optimise inhaled-corticosteroid maintenance, provide a written action plan and
  early follow-up.

## Drug Interactions

- Beta-agonists + non-selective beta-blockers → antagonism; beta-blockers can
  provoke bronchospasm and are generally avoided.
- High-dose/repeated SABA + theophylline or diuretics → additive hypokalaemia;
  monitor potassium.
- Systemic corticosteroids worsen hyperglycaemia — review diabetic patients (see
  `[[conditions/type-2-diabetes]]`).
- Theophylline levels rise with macrolides and fluoroquinolones (narrow
  therapeutic index).

## Red Flags

- Any life-threatening feature: silent chest, cyanosis, exhaustion, drowsiness,
  confusion, or poor respiratory effort.
- SpO2 < 90% despite oxygen, or a rising/normalising PaCO2 in a deteriorating
  patient.
- Failure to respond to initial bronchodilator and steroid therapy.
- Need for escalating bronchodilator frequency → arrange urgent senior/critical
  care review.
