# Amlodipine

## Summary

A long-acting dihydropyridine calcium-channel blocker used widely for
hypertension and chronic stable/vasospastic angina. Once-daily dosing and a
favourable side-effect profile make it a common first-line antihypertensive,
including in `[[conditions/hypertension]]`.

## Indications

- Hypertension (alone or in combination, commonly with an ACE inhibitor/ARB).
- Chronic stable angina and vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina.

## Dosing

- Adults: start 5 mg once daily; titrate to a maximum of 10 mg once daily.
- Elderly or hepatic impairment: start 2.5 mg once daily.
- Can be taken with or without food; effect is sustained over 24 hours.

## Interactions

- Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g. clarithromycin, ketoconazole) raise amlodipine
  levels — monitor for hypotension/oedema.
- Simvastatin: limit to 20 mg/day when co-prescribed (increased myopathy risk).
- Additive hypotension with other antihypertensives and nitrates.
- Avoid combining non-dihydropyridine CCBs in the same regimen.

## Adverse Effects

- Common: ankle/peripheral oedema (dose-related), flushing, headache,
  palpitations, fatigue.
- Less common: gingival hyperplasia, dizziness, nausea.
- Peripheral oedema is not diuretic-responsive; consider dose reduction or
  adding/switching to an ACEi/ARB.

## Pregnancy/Lactation

- Not first-line in pregnancy; use only if the benefit outweighs risk and
  preferred agents (e.g. labetalol, methyldopa, nifedipine) are unsuitable.
- Small amounts pass into breast milk; use with caution while breastfeeding and
  prefer better-characterised alternatives where possible.
